SAR WRITING GUIDELINE – CLARITY

Description:
SAR narratives must be written in clear, concise, and factual language. The purpose of a SAR narrative is to communicate suspicious activity to regulatory authorities such as FinCEN, FIU-India, or the National Crime Agency. The narrative must be self-explanatory such that the reader can understand the suspicious activity without accessing internal bank systems. Ambiguous or poorly written narratives delay investigations and reduce the effectiveness of law enforcement response. Every statement should be supported by observable transaction data, system alerts, or documented customer behavior.

Best Practices:
* Use plain, factual language that avoids jargon, abbreviations, or internal system codes unless defined.
* Reference specific transaction amounts, dates, counterparties, and account numbers to anchor each claim.
* Maintain a chronological flow of events so the reader can follow the progression of suspicious activity.
* Avoid speculative language such as "the customer may be laundering money." Instead, describe the observable behavior and state why it deviates from expected norms.
* Define all monetary amounts with currency identifiers (e.g., INR 50,00,000 or USD 100,000).
* Use active voice to describe the subject's actions (e.g., "The customer deposited" rather than "deposits were made").

SAR WRITING GUIDELINE – OBJECTIVITY

Description:
SAR narratives must present facts objectively and without personal bias, assumptions, or conclusions about the customer's guilt. The role of the compliance analyst is to describe the suspicious activity and explain why it deviates from expected behavior based on the customer's known profile. The determination of criminal intent is the responsibility of law enforcement, not the filing institution. Subjective language or speculation undermines the credibility of the SAR and can expose the institution to legal liability.

Best Practices:
* Present transaction facts and customer profile information as documented in the institution's records.
* Avoid phrases that assume intent, such as "the customer is laundering money." Instead, use language such as "the transaction pattern is inconsistent with the customer's stated business profile."
* Clearly distinguish between observed facts and analytical assessments.
* When stating a reason for suspicion, explain the analytical basis: what the expected behavior would be, what the actual behavior was, and why the deviation is noteworthy.
* Do not include personal opinions, moral judgments, or characterizations of the customer.
* Reference applicable AML typologies and regulatory guidelines to provide analytical context for the suspicion.

SAR WRITING GUIDELINE – STRUCTURE

Description:
A well-structured SAR narrative follows a logical format that guides the reader through the identification and analysis of suspicious activity. Regulatory agencies expect narratives to cover specific elements including the subject's identity, the nature of the suspicious activity, the transaction details, the reason for suspicion, and any actions taken by the institution. A standardized structure ensures consistency across filings, reduces the risk of omitting critical details, and facilitates efficient review by regulators.

Sections to include:

Introduction:
Identify the filing institution, the subject of the report (customer name, account number, account type), the monitoring period, and the alert or trigger that initiated the review.

Description of Activity:
Provide a chronological description of the suspicious activity. Describe what the customer did, including specific transaction types (cash deposits, wire transfers, check disbursements), amounts, dates, and counterparties.

Transaction Analysis:
Summarize the quantitative aspects of the activity. Include total transaction count, total amounts, date ranges, average transaction sizes, and any patterns (e.g., amounts consistently below reporting thresholds). Compare observed activity to the customer's expected profile.

Reason for Suspicion:
Explain why the observed activity is considered suspicious. Reference applicable AML typologies, describe deviations from the customer's expected behavior, note any missing documentation, and identify connections to high-risk indicators (jurisdictions, PEPs, shell companies).

Conclusion:
State the institution's decision to file the SAR. Summarize the key facts and the reason for suspicion. Note any actions taken by the institution, such as account restrictions, enhanced monitoring, or relationship termination.

SAR WRITING GUIDELINE – WHO WHAT WHEN WHERE WHY

Description:
A well-written SAR narrative should answer the following questions: Who conducted the activity, what occurred, when the activity took place, where the transactions occurred, and why the activity is considered suspicious. This framework ensures that the narrative provides a complete picture of the suspicious activity. Regulatory reviewers and law enforcement investigators rely on these five elements to assess the severity and scope of the reported activity.

Best Practices:
* WHO: Identify the subject by name, customer ID, account number, account type, and declared occupation or business.
* WHAT: Describe the specific transactions and activities that triggered the alert (deposits, transfers, withdrawals).
* WHEN: Provide exact dates and time frames for the suspicious activity and the monitoring period.
* WHERE: Identify branch locations, destination countries, beneficiary institutions, and jurisdictions involved.
* WHY: Explain why the activity is considered suspicious by referencing AML typologies, profile deviations, and regulatory requirements.

SAR WRITING GUIDELINE – TRANSACTION DETAIL AND EVIDENCE

Description:
Transaction details are the evidentiary backbone of a SAR narrative. Every assertion about suspicious activity must be supported by specific, verifiable transaction data. Narratives that lack precise figures, dates, and counterparty information are significantly less useful to law enforcement investigators. Analysts must extract and present relevant transaction details in a manner that enables the reader to independently verify the suspicious pattern.

Best Practices:
* Include exact transaction amounts, dates, originator and beneficiary details, and transaction reference numbers where available.
* Present aggregated statistics: total number of transactions, total amount, date range, and average per-transaction amount.
* Identify and describe the specific transactions that most clearly demonstrate the suspicious pattern.
* If the customer was contacted for explanations, document the response (or lack of response) and note whether the explanation aligned with the observed activity.
* Attach or reference supporting documentation such as account statements, KYC records, or alert summaries if the filing format permits.

SAR WRITING GUIDELINE – CUSTOMER PROFILE COMPARISON

Description:
The suspicion of activity often arises from a deviation between the customer's observed transaction behavior and their known profile. The narrative should establish the customer's baseline profile, including declared occupation, expected income, expected transaction types and volumes, and nature of business. Then the narrative should describe how the observed activity departed from this baseline.

Best Practices:
* State the customer's declared occupation, monthly or annual income, nature of business, and account type.
* Describe the customer's historical transaction behavior (average monthly deposits, typical transaction sizes).
* Quantify the deviation: e.g., "The customer's average monthly deposits were INR 1,50,000 over the past 12 months. During the review period, deposits totaled INR 50,00,000 in 7 days — a 3,233% increase."
* Reference any KYC deficiencies: missing documents, incomplete beneficial ownership information, or outdated profile data.
* Note whether the customer was given an opportunity to explain the activity and what response, if any, was received.
